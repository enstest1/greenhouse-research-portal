Banker Plants — Aphidius colemani & Rhopalosiphum padi
COMMERCIAL SCALE — COMPLETE TECHNICAL PROTOCOL
At $800k/year in aphid control costs, a properly designed banker plant programme is the highest-ROI investment in your IPM system. The difference between a system that works and one that gives false confidence while aphids build is almost entirely in the details: grass growth stage at inoculation, aphid instar distribution at colemani release, RH at mummy emergence, and production yield modelling to ensure you have enough parasitoid output per m² of greenhouse. This section covers the full technical protocol at commercial glass greenhouse scale.
The Commercial System — What You Are Actually Building
You grow cereal grass (barley) → establish R. padi (bird cherry oat aphid) colony at GS 21–22 → allow colony to reach stable age-structure (50%+ L3–L4) → inoculate with clean Aphidius colemani → colemani parasitises R. padi → mummies form → adults emerge at RH ≥70% with full wing function → disperse into crop → parasitise Myzus persicae (peppers) and Aphis gossypii (cucumbers) before populations establish.

R. padi is a non-crop aphid — it cannot colonise your cucumbers, peppers, or tomatoes. It is your production substrate, not a pest risk. Colemani parasitises both.

Production target for 23 acres (230,000 m²) at 1 unit per 50 m²: approximately 4,600 banker plant units in full production. This is the scale required for preventive coverage. Most operations start with 500–1,000 units and supplement with commercial sachets — the banker plant system replaces the majority of purchased releases over time.
PART 1 — GRASS: SEED TO OPTIMAL GROWTH STAGE
Your Current Protocol — Technical Assessment
24-hour soak: Good. Imbibition is complete and germination uniformity improves significantly. No change needed.

Sowing: Good. 30–40 seeds per 10cm pot or 150–200g/m² in trays.

Plug introduction at 3 days (sprouting): TOO EARLY. At 3 days, barley is at GS 10–11 — coleoptile emerging, first leaf just appearing. Crown root system is not established. Phloem sap flow to leaves is negligible at this stage. R. padi probes but cannot feed adequately on immature phloem tissue. Colony establishment is slow, thin, and unreliable. You are wasting plugs.

Plug introduction at 7 days: Marginal. GS 11–13 — first leaf fully emerged. Some phloem flow beginning. Colony will establish but slower and at lower final density than optimal. Improvement possible.

OPTIMAL: introduce R. padi at 10–14 days = GS 21–22. At 18–20°C mean temperature, barley reaches GS 21 (2–3 tillers visible, 12–18cm tall) at 10–14 days. At this stage: crown root system established, phloem amino acid content at peak (asparagine, glutamine, threonine — the nutritional drivers of R. padi reproduction), leaf tissue robust enough to support dense colony. Published data shows colony establishment rate at GS 21 is approximately 2.3× that at GS 11.
Grass Growing Protocol — Full Technical Specification
SPECIES: Winter barley (Hordeum vulgare) — optimal. Winter wheat acceptable. Avoid oats (Avena sativa) — slower colony establishment.

SOWING: 30–40 seeds per 10cm pot. Standard peat/perlite 80:20 or similar. Depth: 1.5–2cm. Pre-soak 24h — your current protocol is correct. Germination: 3–4 days at 20°C, 5–6 days at 16°C.

TEMPERATURE: Day 18–22°C / Night 14–16°C. Cooler nights are intentional — they slow elongation and produce compact, tiller-dense grass that is easier to manage and does not lodge under colony weight. Do not be tempted to run warm nights for faster growth — you want tillers, not height.

LIGHT: Minimum 150 µmol m⁻² s⁻¹ PAR, 16h photoperiod. T5 HO fluorescent or full-spectrum LED. Insufficient light produces etiolated, weak-stemmed grass that lodges under aphid colony weight and develops poorly. Light is the main driver of phloem sugar loading — it directly determines the nutritional quality of your banker plant substrate.

HUMIDITY: 55–65% RH. Lower than your crop compartments. Good air movement prevents Entomophthora fungal infection in dense grass planting. A small circulation fan running continuously is sufficient.

HIGH-N FERTILISATION — CRITICAL: Begin applying high-N soluble fertiliser (20-5-10 or equivalent) from day 7 onwards at 200–250 ppm N. Apply every 3 days by fertigation or foliar. High N drives the amino acid content of phloem sap — specifically free amino acids asparagine and glutamine — that make the grass nutritious for R. padi. Without high N, your aphid colony will be thin, slow-reproducing, and unstable. This is the single most commonly neglected step in commercial banker plant programmes.

TARGET GROWTH STAGE AT APHID INTRODUCTION: GS 21–22 (2–3 tillers visible, 12–18cm tall). At 18–20°C: approximately Day 10–14.

PART 2 — R. PADI COLONY: ESTABLISHMENT TO PRODUCTION-READY
R. padi Biology — Development Times and Instar Reference
R. padi reproduces parthenogenetically in the greenhouse — all females, no males, continuous asexual reproduction. Understanding development times lets you predict exactly when your colony will reach the instar distribution required for colemani inoculation.

Development time nymph to adult:
At 15°C: 14–16 days
At 18°C: 10–12 days
At 20°C: 8–10 days
At 22°C: 6–8 days
At 24°C: 6–7 days

Instar size reference (critical for colemani host selection):
L1: 0.4–0.7mm — newly born. TOO SMALL for colemani parasitism. Colemani will probe and reject.
L2: 0.7–1.1mm — borderline. Colemani attempts but success rate <20%.
L3: 1.1–1.7mm — OPTIMAL. Wing pads visible on wingless morphs. Colemani success rate 70–85%.
L4: 1.7–2.3mm — very good. Colemani success rate 60–75%.
Adult: 1.7–2.8mm — acceptable. Colemani will parasitise but mummy development is slower and adult emergence rate slightly reduced.

Key principle: colemani females use antennation to assess host quality before ovipositing. Aphids below L3 are rejected because the egg cannot develop in insufficient host body mass. If your colony is predominantly L1–L2 when you release colemani, you will see probing behaviour without oviposition — this looks like the system is working but produces no mummies.
Your Plug Method — Optimised Protocol
Your method of using pre-infested plugs is more sophisticated than direct inoculation from a dry aphid supply. The optimisation is in the plug quality and timing.

PLUG PREPARATION (at your aphid rearing unit):
Pre-infest plugs or grass leaf material at least 5–7 days before intended introduction. Start with 20–30 adult R. padi per plug. By day 5–7 the plug colony will contain adults + L1–L2 nymphs. This is the correct plug stage for introduction — the adults immediately colonise the grass while the nymphs develop in situ.

INTRODUCTION TIMING:
Introduce plugs when grass is at GS 21–22 (Day 10–14 from sowing). Not at sprouting (Day 3). Not at first leaf (Day 7). At GS 21 only.

INTRODUCTION DENSITY:
20–30 adults per 10cm pot or per 0.1m² tray section. At 20°C this colony will reach 200–400 individuals within 10–14 days through parthenogenetic reproduction.

POST-INTRODUCTION CONDITIONS:
Temperature: 18–22°C. RH: 60–70% (not above 75% — Entomophthora risk). Light: 16h. Continue high-N fertigation every 3 days. Check daily from Day 5 after introduction for signs of colony establishment (aphids visible on all tillers, not just the plug material).

TIMELINE TO COLEMANI INOCULATION AT 20°C:
Day 0: plug introduction at GS 21–22
Days 1–4: founder colony establishing, mostly adults and early L1
Days 5–7: L1 nymphs appearing across grass
Days 8–10: first L1→L2 moults
Days 10–13: first L2→L3 moults — begin daily instar assessments
Days 12–15: stable age-structure reached (50%+ L3–L4, 200+ aphids per pot) — INOCULATE COLEMANI

At lower temperatures (16–18°C): add 3–5 days to each stage.

PART 3 — COLEMANI: INOCULATION, EMERGENCE, AND RH
THE RH REQUIREMENT — THE MOST IMPORTANT SINGLE VARIABLE
When a colemani adult emerges from the mummy, the wings must expand hydraulically using haemolymph pressure within 15–20 minutes of emergence. If the cuticle hardens before full expansion — due to dry air — the wings remain permanently crumpled. The adult is alive but cannot fly. It cannot search the greenhouse. It is a failed investment.

Published RH thresholds:
Below 55% RH: >40% of emerging adults have wing deformities. Severely impaired flight.
55–65% RH: 15–25% deformity rate. Reduced flight range.
65–70% RH: <5% deformity. Near-normal wing expansion.
Above 70% RH: Full wing expansion, normal flight behaviour, normal searching radius.

Your target: maintain RH ≥70% at the banker plant level at all times, especially during morning hours when temperature rises and mummy emergence occurs. Most emergence happens in the 2–4 hours after temperature begins rising in the morning — this is your highest-risk window for wing failure if RH drops.

Practical implication: If your banker plants are positioned in your cucumber compartment where you are actively managing HD down to 1.5–3 g/m³ (which at 22°C = RH ~60–65%), you may be impairing colemani wing function every morning precisely when emergence is happening. Consider a dedicated banker plant nurse compartment maintained at 70–80% RH, or position banker plants in your pepper compartment which you now know runs at RH 91–92%.
Colemani Inoculation Protocol — Full Sequence
SOURCE — USE CLEAN COMMERCIAL STOCK:
For your initial inoculation, use commercially produced colemani from Koppert, Biobest, or BioLine. These are guaranteed clean (unparasitised by hyperparasitoids) and have known parasitism rates. Do NOT propagate colemani from adults caught in your crop — field populations carry hyperparasitoids and may have been through many generations of inbreeding which reduces fecundity. Refresh with commercial stock every 8–12 weeks to maintain genetic diversity and parasitism efficiency.

INOCULATION TRIGGER — INSTAR ASSESSMENT:
Before releasing colemani, count the instar distribution on 5 tillers across 3 pots. Target for inoculation:
L3+L4: ≥50% of total colony
Colony density: ≥150 aphids per pot
L1: ≤20% (healthy incoming generation present but not dominant)
Adults: 15–25% (stable reproductive base)
If L1+L2 exceeds 50% — wait 3–5 more days at 20°C and reassess.

INOCULATION RATE ON BANKER PLANTS:
Initial: 3–5 colemani adults per 10cm pot (approximately 1 female per 30–50 aphids in the colony). This sounds very low but each female lays 100–300 eggs over 7–14 days. At 30% parasitism rate, a colony of 200 aphids produces 60 mummies per cycle.
Weekly top-up: 1–2 adults per pot to maintain parasitism pressure and prevent colemani population aging.

RELEASE CONDITIONS:
Time: morning, 2–3 hours after lights on / sunrise. Temperature rising, RH still above 70%.
Method: open container at grass level, allow adults to walk onto grass independently. Do not tap or shake.
Do not release when RH <65% or temperature >28°C.

EXPECTED TIMELINE AFTER INOCULATION:
Days 1–3: colemani searching and ovipositing
Days 4–7: L3–L4 aphids becoming mummies (visible as rounded, papery, golden-tan)
Days 7–10 (at 20°C): first adult emergence from mummies
Days 10–14: second generation in production, banker plant system self-sustaining

The mummy confirmation check: at Day 6–8 after inoculation you should see 10–20 mummies per 100 aphids. If you see zero mummies at Day 10 — problem. See troubleshooting section.

For moderate aphid pressure (5–20 per plant detected):
Increase to 5 adults per 50m² per week → approximately 1,650 units or supplement with commercial sachet releases.

The staggered sowing schedule (critical for continuous production):
Sow new banker plant batches every 10–14 days. Each batch takes 24–28 days from sowing to first adult colemani emergence. Running 3 overlapping batches at any time ensures you never have a production gap when grass goes past GS 31 (jointing) and colony declines.
Spatial Placement Design for 23 Acres
Placement density: 1 unit per 50m² for preventive. 1 per 25m² when aphid pressure is detected in an area.

Position within bay: Place units at crop level, not overhead. Colemani moves upward and outward into canopy from ground level. Placement at bench or floor height gives best dispersal trajectory into the crop.

Distribution along bay length: In 100m bays, place units every 10–15m. Colemani searching radius at optimal conditions is 10–15m. Gaps larger than this create dispersal voids where aphid colonies can establish before colemani arrives.

Compartment prioritisation:
Peppers — highest priority. Myzus persicae is the primary species and colemani is most effective against it. Your high-RH pepper compartment (91–92%) is actually ideal for mummy emergence — full wing expansion guaranteed.
Cucumbers — second priority. Aphis gossypii is less efficiently parasitised by colemani than M. persicae. May require supplementation with Aphidoletes aphidimyza (gall midge) which is highly effective against A. gossypii.
TOV tomatoes — moderate priority. Both M. persicae and Macrosiphum euphorbiae present. Colemani handles M. persicae well; A. ervi is better for M. euphorbiae if it becomes a problem.

PART 5 — WEEKLY MONITORING PROTOCOL
Weekly Banker Plant Assessment — What to Measure and Record
At commercial scale, the banker plant programme must be monitored with the same rigour as crop data. What is not measured cannot be optimised.

GRASS HEALTH CHECKS (weekly per batch):
— Height: 20–40cm optimal. Taller = lodging risk
— Colour: mid-green. Yellowing = N deficiency, apply fertigation immediately
— Disease: check base for powdery mildew or grey mould. Fan running?
— Growth stage: still at GS 21–31? Past GS 31 = replace within 5 days
— Tiller count: 4–8 per plant. Fewer = poor establishment or cold stress

APHID COLONY ASSESSMENT (weekly, 5 tillers per 3 pots):
— Total count per tiller
— Instar distribution (%): target 50%+ L3–L4 during production phase
— Colony density target: 100–250 aphids per tiller
— Below 50: reinoculate that unit within 24 hours
— Above 400: thin by removing 2–3 tillers or increase colemani
— Entomophthora check: any aphids stuck to leaves with grey appearance?

MUMMY ASSESSMENT (weekly — the most important check):
— Count mummies per 100 aphids on 3 tillers per pot
— Target parasitism rate: 30–45% (30–45 mummies per 100 aphids)
— Above 60%: risk of colony collapse — reduce colemani releases
— Below 15%: colemani inoculation insufficient or failing — investigate
— Mummy colour: golden-tan = healthy colemani. Dark brown-black = possible hyperparasitism
— Emergence holes: present = system working. Absent on old mummies = investigate
— Hole size: small circular = colemani. Large ragged = hyperparasitoid

CROP PARASITISM MONITORING (weekly, 20 plants per compartment):
— Count aphids per standard sampling protocol
— Count mummified aphids in crop per 100 aphids found
— Target: ≥20% parasitism of any detected crop aphids
— Below 10%: banker plant dispersal inadequate — check placement and RH
— Zero mummies despite crop aphids: system failure — immediate investigation
PART 6 — FAILURE MODES, DIAGNOSIS, AND RECOVERY
Troubleshooting — Commercial Scale Failure Modes
FAILURE: Colemani present on banker plants, probing aphids, but zero or very few mummies
Check 1 (most likely): instar distribution. Weigh 20 aphids visually — are most L1–L2? Colemani is rejecting undersized hosts. Do not release more colemani. Wait 4–6 days for colony to mature. Next time: delay colemani release until L3 dominance confirmed.
Check 2: RH. Below 65%? Colemani are present but searching inefficiently with partially impaired wings. They can land on aphids but orientation is disrupted. Raise RH to 70%+ in banker plant area.
Check 3: recent organic inputs. Pyrethrin, neem, spinosad all have repellent or toxic effects on colemani lasting 3–7 days after application. Check spray records.

FAILURE: Mummies forming, correct colour, but no adults emerging
Cut open 10–15 mummies. Internal content check:
Golden-tan larva/pupa = healthy colemani. May just need more time (cool temperature?) — check thermal model.
Dark brown larva, larger than colemani = hyperparasitoid (Adialytus, Asaphes). Emergency protocol: destroy affected batch, trace incoming plant material source, source clean replacement R. padi from certified supplier, quarantine incoming material for 14 days minimum.

FAILURE: R. padi colony crashing despite adequate grass
Check A: Entomophthora fungal epizootic. Look for aphids with grey fluffy appearance stuck to leaf surface — these are mycosed aphids releasing spores. Common at sustained RH >80%. Reduce humidity to 60–70% in banker plant area immediately. A single Entomophthora epizootic can collapse a colony in 48–72 hours. If detected early, isolate affected units, increase air movement.
Check B: Parasitism overload. Count mummy rate — above 60%? Your colemani population has become too dense relative to the colony. Reduce weekly top-up releases and move some banker plant units further from high colemani density zones.
Check C: Grass past GS 31. Phloem sap quality changes at jointing — amino acid composition shifts unfavourably for R. padi. Replace grass immediately with new GS 21–22 batch.
Check D: N deficiency in grass. Yellow grass = poor phloem quality = colony declines slowly over 7–10 days. Apply 250 ppm N fertigation and reassess in 5 days.

FAILURE: System performing on banker plants but aphid outbreak in specific crop compartment
This is almost always a spatial coverage gap or a species mismatch.
Spatial gap: measure distance from nearest active banker plant unit to outbreak location. If >15m, dispersal is insufficient. Move units closer and add direct commercial sachet releases in affected zone immediately.
Species mismatch: if outbreak is in cucumber and species is confirmed A. gossypii, colemani may be present but underperforming on this host. Add Aphidoletes aphidimyza releases directly into the affected zone — Aphidoletes is highly effective against A. gossypii colony outbreaks that colemani alone cannot suppress fast enough.
RH issue: check RH at time of main mummy emergence (early morning). If compartment RH drops below 65% at that window, you are consistently producing impaired colemani that can walk but not fly properly — they never reach the remote areas of the bay.

PART 7 — COMMERCIAL SCALE: NURSE COMPARTMENT, QUALITY CONTROL, AND ROI
The Nurse Compartment Concept — Why You Need One at 23 Acres
At your scale, producing banker plants inside crop compartments creates a fundamental conflict: optimal conditions for R. padi colony establishment (60–70% RH, 18–22°C, high N, 16h light) are not the same as optimal conditions for colemani mummy emergence (≥70% RH, 20–24°C). Running banker plants in your cucumber compartment where you are actively managing HD down to 1.5–3 g/m³ compromises wing expansion every morning at exactly the moment emergence occurs.

NURSE COMPARTMENT SPECIFICATION (100–150 m² dedicated space):
Temperature: 20–22°C constant. Stable temperature = predictable emergence timing.
RH: 70–78% maintained at all times. This is the single non-negotiable parameter.
Light: 150–200 µmol m⁻² s⁻¹ PAR, 16h photoperiod. Full-spectrum LED at bench height.
Air movement: gentle circulation only. Not drying. Not stagnant.
Ventilation: minimal. Maintain RH, do not exchange air aggressively.
N fertigation: 200–250 ppm N, every 3 days on all grass in production.

WORKFLOW:
Batches are sown, grown to GS 21–22, inoculated with R. padi, colony established, colemani inoculated — all in the nurse compartment. Once mummies are visible and forming (Day 28–32 from sowing), units are moved into the crop compartment for dispersal. At this point the mummies need ≥70% RH for emergence, which your pepper compartment (91–92% RH) provides naturally. Your cucumber compartments (lower RH) need supplementary humidification around banker plant units OR mummies must be partially emerged before placement in the drier sections.

STAGGERED PRODUCTION SCHEDULE (to maintain continuous output):
Sow a new batch every 10–14 days. Each batch takes 28–35 days from sowing to producing flying adults. You need 3 overlapping batches running simultaneously at all times. When batch 1 goes past GS 31 (jointing) and colony quality declines, batch 2 is in peak production and batch 3 is in establishment phase. No production gaps.
Colemani Quality Assessment Before Deployment — The Pre-Release Check
Commercial colemani sachets (Koppert, Biobest, BioLine) have stated emergence rates but these degrade with temperature excursions during shipping. Deploying a compromised batch into 23 acres of glass greenhouse means you have no parasitoid coverage while believing you do. This is how $800k gets spent — apparent biocontrol activity with insufficient actual output.

EMERGENCE TEST PROTOCOL (takes 24–36 hours):
On receipt of each commercial batch, remove 20–30 mummies from the sachet.
Place in a clean petri dish or small container with a moist cotton ball (for humidity).
Hold at 22°C, 75% RH for 24–36 hours.
Count emerged adults with wings fully expanded vs total mummies.

Acceptance criteria:
≥75% emergence rate: batch is good, deploy as stated rate
60–74%: batch is marginal, increase deployment rate by 30% to compensate
Below 60%: batch is compromised, contact supplier, do not rely on this batch as sole source

Wing expansion check:
Of the adults that emerge, examine 10 under a hand lens or phone magnifier. Wings should be flat, fully extended, equal length. Any wings that are crumpled, held at an angle, or shorter than the abdomen = wing deformity from inadequate RH at emergence. Even 10% deformity in a commercial batch indicates temperature stress during production or shipping.

Documentation:
Record batch number, supplier, date received, emergence rate, and wing quality score. Over time this data tells you which suppliers have consistent quality and which are variable.
Genetic Refreshment Schedule — Preventing Inbreeding Decline
This is one of the least-discussed but most commercially damaging failures in year-round banker plant programmes. When you propagate colemani internally from your own banker plants without introducing outside genetic material, each successive generation has a slightly higher inbreeding coefficient. By generation 10–12 (approximately 10–14 weeks at 20°C) you can expect 15–30% reduction in:
— Parasitism rate per female per day
— Host-searching activity (flight distance, searching time)
— Fecundity (eggs per female over lifetime)

The insidious part is that the system still appears to be working — you see mummies, you see adult emergence — but the per-unit parasitoid output is quietly declining. Your banker plant coverage requirement creeps upward and you compensate by buying more commercial sachets without diagnosing the root cause.

Refreshment protocol:
Every 8–10 weeks: purchase a fresh commercial colemani batch from your supplier.
Introduce these adults (after quality check) directly onto 20–30% of your banker plant units alongside your resident population.
This does not require destroying your resident population — genetic mixing happens naturally when both populations are present on the same banker plants.
Rotate suppliers annually if possible — different production colonies have different genetic backgrounds.
Aphidoletes aphidimyza — The Critical Addition for Your Cucumber Sections
This is likely your largest single control gap at $800k/year if a significant portion of that spend is in cucumber.

Colemani parasitises Aphis gossypii (cucumber aphid) at 40–60% efficiency — less than half its efficiency against Myzus persicae. A. gossypii also reproduces rapidly and builds dense waxy colonies that physically deter colemani searching behaviour. In established A. gossypii colonies (>50 per plant), colemani alone cannot achieve curative control fast enough to prevent yield loss.

Aphidoletes aphidimyza biology:
A gall midge (small fly). Adults lay eggs near aphid colonies at night. Larvae hatch and consume 3–50 aphids per larval stage by injecting a paralysing secretion. Life cycle: egg to adult in 14–21 days at 20°C. Requires ≥9h darkness per cycle for oviposition — photoperiod-sensitive. If you run 24h lighting anywhere, Aphidoletes will not reproduce in those areas.

Integration protocol with colemani:
Colemani: continuous preventive from banker plants (targets adults and nymphs before colony establishment)
Aphidoletes: targeted curative when colonies of >20 A. gossypii per plant are detected (targets dense colonies that colemani cannot suppress fast enough)
The two species are compatible — they occupy different ecological niches and do not compete.

Release rate: 1–2 Aphidoletes per m² in affected cucumber sections. One release per week for 2–3 weeks when a hotspot is detected.

Banker plant equivalent for Aphidoletes:
Aphidoletes does not use a banker plant system — it requires live aphid colonies in the crop as breeding habitat. You maintain it by ensuring colemani pressure never completely eliminates aphid colonies in cucumber — a small managed aphid presence supports a resident Aphidoletes population. This is counterintuitive but is the Dutch commercial organic cucumber protocol.
Soft-Chemical Compatibility — What Destroys Your Programme
At $800k spend you have almost certainly applied some organically permitted active materials. Some of these have significant residual activity against colemani. Applying them without accounting for residual effects means you are releasing banker plant adults into a compartment where they die before they can establish parasitism.

HIGH RISK — avoid near active banker plant programme:
Pyrethrin (natural pyrethrum): toxic to colemani on contact, repellent for 5–7 days post-application. If you must use: apply in evening, wait 7 days before placing banker plant units in that area.
Spinosad: kills colemani on contact, residual repellency 3–5 days. Incompatible with active banker plants in zone.
Azadirachtin (neem): sublethal effects on colemani — reduces fecundity and searching behaviour for 4–7 days even without direct contact. Residue on plant surfaces is the risk.

MODERATE RISK — use with caution and timing:
Kaolin clay: physical barrier that interferes with colemani landing and host-finding when leaves are heavily coated. Effect disappears once clay wears off (7–14 days).
Potassium bicarbonate: low direct toxicity to colemani. Can be used but apply when banker plants are not in the zone if possible.
Copper sulphate: low risk at label rates. Timing matters more than avoidance.

LOW RISK — generally compatible:
Sulphur (except heat sulphur vaporisers): compatible at label rates if temperature not extreme.
Beauveria bassiana: generally compatible with colemani adults at commercial rates.
Bacillus thuringiensis: compatible, no effect on colemani.
Silicon products: compatible.

Reference: Koppert Side Effects database (www.side-effects.koppert.nl) lists every commercial product against every benefical organism with residual period data. This is the most important free resource for your integrated programme management.
Aphid Resistance Assessment — Why Your $800k May Be Partly Wasted
If organically permitted contact actives (pyrethrins, spinosad, azadirachtin) have been used repeatedly on your aphid populations over multiple seasons, resistance may have developed. Resistance shows as:
— Aphid populations rebounding faster than expected after treatment
— Increasing doses needed for equivalent knockdown
— Colemani mummy counts looking healthy but population decline slower than models predict
— Colonies that appear "sticky" — mummies visible but surviving aphids continue reproducing

Testing protocol:
Collect 100+ live M. persicae from pepper sections and 100+ A. gossypii from cucumber sections.
Submit to Koppert or Biobest resistance testing service, or contact IOBC working group on pesticide resistance in beneficial arthropods.
A resistant population requires a completely non-chemical approach for 1–2 full seasons to allow resistance allele frequency to decline before actives are useful again.
If resistance is confirmed: full biological programme with zero chemical intervention becomes both a necessity and an opportunity to reset.

How greenhouse computers translate setpoints into physical actions — vent position, screen deployment, pipe temperature. P-bands define how aggressively the system responds. Understanding these means you can tune Priva behavior rather than just setting numbers.
P-Band Concept
A proportional band defines the range over which a controller output moves from 0% to 100%. Narrow P-band = aggressive response, risk of oscillation. Wide P-band = slow response, stable but sluggish. For vents: a 2°C P-band means the vent goes from 0% to 100% open across a 2°C temperature deviation from setpoint. The same logic applies to screens and pipe temperature.

Stanghellini Canopy Resistance — Full Form
E = (Δ·Rn + ρ·Cp·VPD/ra) / (Δ + γ·(1 + rc/ra))
Δ = slope of saturation vapour pressure · ρCp = heat capacity of air · ra = aerodynamic resistance · rc = canopy resistance · γ = psychrometric constant

rc is what stomata control. Everything you do with temperature and HD is trying to manage rc. When rc is low, stomata are open, transpiration is high, calcium moves. When rc rises — stress, low light, high humidity — the plant closes down.
Integral Temperature Control — T-Sum Strategy
T-sum = Σ(T_actual − T_setpoint) over rolling 24–48h window
Instead of fixed setpoints, Priva tracks cumulative temperature deviation. Run cold overnight, compensate with a warmer morning. The plant sees the same 24h mean regardless of distribution. Decouples energy saving from crop development rate.

The Two Goals of a Greenhouse Incentive System
SPEED — volume of work completed per hour (picking rate, training speed)
QUALITY — work done correctly (clean cuts, correct fruit selection, gentle handling)

INCENTIVE SYSTEM DESIGN FRAMEWORK
Recommended Framework for Your Operation This Year
STEP 1 — Establish baselines first (weeks 1–2)
Time each task for each worker before any incentive is introduced. This is your fair reference point. Task × crop × worker = baseline rate. Without this data, any incentive will feel arbitrary.

STEP 2 — Define quality floors (non-negotiable)
Picking: no fruit left behind over spec · stem scars clean (no tearing) · gentle bin placement
Training: correct node, no broken laterals
Pruning: clean cuts, correct height
Quality floor = if below, worker returns to hourly only. Not punitive, just the condition for incentive eligibility.

STEP 3 — Speed bonus above threshold
Set threshold at ~90% of baseline. Above threshold, worker earns a percentage bonus on pay per additional % of output. Example: threshold = 100 kg/hr picked. Above 110 = 5% pay bonus. Above 120 = 10% pay bonus. Cap at reasonable level to prevent rushing.

STEP 4 — Separate crop work from picking
Picking incentives and crop work (training, pruning, deleafing) incentives should be separate. Crop work quality has a 2–4 week lag before visible outcome — workers need to understand this connection explicitly before incentives on crop work make sense to them.

STEP 5 — Weekly transparent reporting
Post each worker's rate vs threshold weekly. Workers who cannot see their own performance relative to the target cannot improve. Transparency also prevents perception of favouritism which destroys incentive system trust faster than anything else.
